\begin{abstract}
\noindent
For my CSCI~3010U course project, I built a two-dimensional billiards
simulation using Python and the Pygame library.
The simulation places eight balls on a table and models their motion,
wall bouncing, and mutual collisions using real physics principles.
Ball positions are updated with the Euler method for numerical
integration, wall bouncing works by reversing the perpendicular velocity
component, and ball--ball collisions are resolved using Newton's Law
of Restitution with an impulse-based approach.
A linear damping model simulates friction on the table surface.
Three controlled experiments were conducted to study how the restitution
coefficient and damping coefficient affect the total kinetic energy and
total momentum of the system over time.
All results matched theoretical predictions from physics, confirming
that the simulation is physically valid.
\end{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Billiards, Euler method, numerical integration,
collision detection, Newton's Law of Restitution, damping,
kinetic energy, conservation of momentum

\section{Introduction}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
Computer simulations allow us to study physical systems that would be
difficult, expensive, or dangerous to test in real life.
In my project, I chose to simulate a two-dimensional billiards table
because billiards is a well-understood physical system where the results
of a simulation can easily be verified by visual inspection and by
comparing measurements against known physics formulas.
I also chose billiards because the problem connects directly to the
simulation techniques taught in CSCI~3010U: solving ordinary
differential equations with a numerical integrator, detecting and
resolving rigid-body collisions, and analysing energy and momentum
as observable quantities that validate the simulation.
The simulation I built handles four main tasks:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Updating each ball's position at every frame using the Euler
        method.
  \item Detecting and responding to ball--wall collisions by reversing
        the appropriate velocity component.
  \item Detecting and resolving ball--ball collisions using conservation
        of momentum and Newton's Law of Restitution.
  \item Applying a linear damping model to simulate friction on the
        table surface.
\end{enumerate}
Beyond building the simulation, I also ran three quantitative experiments
to test whether the system behaves as physics theory predicts.
This included tests for energy conservation in elastic collisions,
exponential energy decay under friction, and momentum conservation
during ball--ball collisions.

\section{Related Work}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
Billiard ball simulation has a long history in both physics education
and computer graphics.
Gould, Tobochnik, and Christian~\cite{gould2007}, whose textbook is
used in this course, present billiards as a standard benchmark for
particle collision simulations and discuss the importance of using
accurate numerical integration and correct collision response.
Their work demonstrates that even a simplified 2D model can exhibit
rich physical behaviour including chaotic trajectories and energy
exchange between particles.
In the computer graphics and game development literature,
Ericson~\cite{ericson2004} describes the impulse-based collision
response method in detail.
This is the same method I used in my project: computing a collision
normal, projecting relative velocities onto it, and applying an
impulse to conserve momentum according to a chosen restitution
coefficient.
The approach is widely used in real-time game physics engines such
as \textit{Box2D} and \textit{Bullet Physics}~\cite{bullet}.
Earlier billiards simulations in academic settings
(such as the ``Billiard'' project referenced in the CSCI~3010U course
list of past projects) have generally focused on implementing the
basic physics correctly.
My project goes further by including a systematic experimental analysis
of how restitution and damping parameters affect system energy
and momentum, which adds a quantitative validation component
that is less common in course-level implementations.
The Pygame library~\cite{pygame} is used for rendering.
While Pygame does not include a built-in physics engine,
this is intentional: the goal of the project is to implement the
physics from scratch using the mathematical models from lectures,
rather than relying on an existing engine.

\section{Methodology}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Numerical Integration --- the Euler Method}
Each ball's motion is governed by ordinary differential equations.
To simulate this on a computer, I discretise time with a step
$\Delta t$ and update positions using the \textbf{Euler method}:
\begin{align}
  x(t+\Delta t) &= x(t) + v_x(t)\cdot\Delta t \\
  y(t+\Delta t) &= y(t) + v_y(t)\cdot\Delta t
\end{align}
The Euler method is a first-order integrator.
I chose it because it is simple to implement and fast enough for
a real-time simulation running at 120 frames per second.
Its main drawback is that it introduces a small numerical error
at each step: the predicted position drifts slightly from the
true analytical solution.
At $\Delta t = 1/120\,\text{s}$, this error is negligible and
does not affect the visible realism of the simulation.
For scientific applications requiring higher precision,
a fourth-order Runge--Kutta (RK4) method would be preferred.
\subsection*{Damping --- Simulating Friction}
I model friction by multiplying each velocity component by a
decay factor at every time step:
\begin{equation}
  v(t+\Delta t) = v(t)\cdot(1 - \lambda\,\Delta t)
\end{equation}
where $\lambda \geq 0$ is the damping coefficient.
This produces an exponential decay of speed over time, consistent
with a linear drag force.
Setting $\lambda = 0$ gives frictionless rolling; larger values
bring balls to rest faster.
\subsection*{Ball--Wall Collisions}
When a ball's edge crosses a table boundary, I reverse the velocity
component perpendicular to that wall:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item Left or right wall: $v_x \leftarrow -v_x$
  \item Top or bottom wall: $v_y \leftarrow -v_y$
\end{itemize}
I also reposition the ball to remove any overlap with the boundary.
This prevents \textit{tunnelling} (a ball passing through the wall
in a single time step at high speed).
All wall collisions are perfectly elastic: no kinetic energy is lost.
\subsection*{Ball--Ball Collision Detection and Response}
Two balls are colliding when:
\begin{equation}
  d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2} \;<\; r_1 + r_2
\end{equation}
To resolve the collision, I compute a unit normal $\hat{n}$ from
ball~1 toward ball~2, and the relative velocity along that normal:
\begin{equation}
  dv_n = (\mathbf{v}_1 - \mathbf{v}_2)\cdot\hat{n}
\end{equation}
If $dv_n \leq 0$ the balls are already separating and no action is
needed.
Otherwise the impulse magnitude is:
\begin{equation}
  J = \frac{-(1+e)\cdot dv_n}{\dfrac{1}{m_1}+\dfrac{1}{m_2}}
  \label{eq:impulse}
\end{equation}
where $e \in [0,1]$ is the coefficient of restitution.
The impulse acts along the collision normal $\hat{n}$ and
redistributes velocity between the two balls ---
a faster ball slows down and the slower ball speeds up,
exactly as in a real collision.
The velocity updates are:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{v}_1 &\mathrel{+}= (J/m_1)\,\hat{n}, &
  \mathbf{v}_2 &\mathrel{-}= (J/m_2)\,\hat{n}
\end{align}
When $e=1$ (elastic) kinetic energy is conserved.
When $e<1$ (inelastic) some energy is dissipated each collision.
\subsection*{Code Structure}
The project consists of four Python files, all available at the
GitHub repository listed at the top of this report:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{\texttt{ball.py}} --- \texttt{Ball} class with Euler
        update, wall collision, and kinetic energy methods.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{physics.py}} --- \texttt{resolve\_ball\_collision()}
        function implementing Equation~\ref{eq:impulse}.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{main.py}} --- Pygame window (1130$\times$710 px,

  \item \textbf{\texttt{analysis.py}} --- Headless experiment runner

\end{itemize}

\section{Results}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
I ran three experiments using \texttt{analysis.py}, simulating
12 seconds of table time without a display window.
All experiments use the same fixed initial conditions (random seed)
so results are directly comparable.
Figure~\ref{fig:results} shows all three plots.
\subsection*{Experiment 1 --- Effect of Restitution on Kinetic Energy}
\textbf{Setup:}
$e \in \{1.0,\;0.7,\;0.4\}$, damping $\lambda = 0$.
\textbf{Result:}
At $e=1.0$ the total kinetic energy is constant throughout --
elastic collisions conserve KE exactly.
At $e=0.7$ and $e=0.4$, the graph shows step-like decreases;
each step corresponds to one ball--ball collision dissipating energy.
Lower $e$ produces larger drops per step.
\textbf{Interpretation:}
The simulation correctly implements Newton's Law of Restitution.
The energy drop per collision grows as $e$ decreases, which matches
Equation~\ref{eq:impulse}.
\subsection*{Experiment 2 --- Effect of Damping on Kinetic Energy}
\textbf{Setup:}
$\lambda \in \{0.0,\;0.2,\;0.6\}$, restitution $e = 1.0$.
\textbf{Result:}
At $\lambda=0$ energy is constant.
At $\lambda=0.2$ energy decays smoothly along an exponential curve.
At $\lambda=0.6$ the decay is steep enough that balls stop within
approximately four seconds.
\textbf{Interpretation:}
The smooth exponential decay matches the analytical solution of the
linear damping ODE, confirming that the friction model is correct.
\subsection*{Experiment 3 --- Total Momentum Over Time}
\textbf{Setup:}
Elastic collisions, no damping; momentum magnitude tracked for 12\,s.
\textbf{Result:}
The momentum magnitude shows sudden discrete jumps.
Between jumps the value is stable.
\textbf{Interpretation:}
Each jump corresponds to a ball hitting a wall (an external force).
Ball--ball collisions produce no jump because the impulse each ball
receives is equal and opposite, so the total momentum is unchanged.
This confirms Newton's Third Law and conservation of linear momentum
within an isolated sub-system.

\section{Discussion and Conclusions}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Discussion}
All three experiments produced results consistent with physics theory,
which tells me the simulation is working correctly.
One limitation worth noting is the \textbf{Euler method's numerical
error}.
Because the Euler method is first-order, it accumulates a small
position error at each time step.
I verified this is not a practical problem in my simulation by
confirming that kinetic energy stays exactly flat under elastic,
frictionless conditions (Experiment~1).
If higher precision were required (for example, for scientific
computation rather than real-time rendering), replacing Euler with
RK4 would be the logical next step.
The \textbf{brute-force collision detection} ($O(n^2)$ pairs checked
every frame) is another limitation.
For 8 balls this is 28 checks per frame and is fast enough.
For a large number of balls a spatial partitioning structure
(e.g., a uniform grid or quadtree) would be necessary.
\subsection*{Conclusions}
This project gave me practical experience applying the core simulation
techniques from CSCI~3010U to a physical system.
I implemented:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item The Euler method for real-time numerical integration.
  \item Elastic wall collision response with positional correction.
  \item Impulse-based ball--ball collision using Newton's Law
        of Restitution.
  \item Linear damping to model surface friction.
\end{itemize}
The three quantitative experiments confirmed that these models
are correct: elastic collisions conserved energy,
lower restitution dissipated more energy per hit,
higher damping produced faster exponential decay,
and momentum was conserved between ball--ball collisions.
Future improvements I would consider include proper pocket detection,
RK4 integration, and spatial partitioning for scalability.
This project helped me see how the equations from lectures translate
directly into working, physically accurate simulation code.
